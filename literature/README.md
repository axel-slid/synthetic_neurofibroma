# Neurofibroma Growth and Physical Modeling Synthesis

Generated: 2026-06-20

NF here means NF1-associated neurofibroma unless stated otherwise.

## High-level answer

Neurofibroma growth is best modeled as a Schwann-lineage tumor process that is constrained by nerve anatomy and amplified by a living microenvironment. Plexiform neurofibromas are not usually described as "spreading" by metastasis. They expand locally along nerves, nerve roots, and nerve plexuses, with morbidity coming from local mass effect, infiltration around nerve fascicles, vascular/soft-tissue involvement, and occasional malignant transformation to MPNST.

## Where it is from

The genetic starting point is loss of neurofibromin function from NF1, a tumor-suppressor/RASopathy gene. Mechanistically, this supports modeling NF1 loss as altered RAS/MAPK-centered signaling rather than as a generic proliferation rate: [A RASopathy gene commonly mutated in cancer: the neurofibromatosis type 1 tumour suppressor.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25877329/) (25877329) and [Neurofibromatosis type 1: Fundamental insights into cell signalling and cancer.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26860753/) (26860753).

The initiating tumor compartment is Schwann lineage. Foundational mouse genetics tied neurofibroma formation to NF1 loss in Schwann cells plus a permissive environment: [Neurofibromas in NF1: Schwann cell origin and role of tumor environment.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11988578/) (11988578). Later work separated subtype-specific origins. Dermal/cutaneous tumors involve susceptible skin-derived precursor or Schwann-lineage populations: [Cell of origin and microenvironment contribution for NF1-associated dermal neurofibromas.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19427294/) (19427294). Plexiform neurofibromas are strongly linked to embryonic Schwann-lineage cells in nerve roots and developmental windows of vulnerability: [Susceptible stages in Schwann cells for NF1-associated plexiform neurofibroma development.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21551250/) (21551250) and [Cells of origin in the embryonic nerve roots for NF1-associated plexiform neurofibroma.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25446898/) (25446898). Timing and location of NF1 loss matter for subtype: [Spatiotemporal Loss of NF1 in Schwann Cell Lineage Leads to Different Types of Cutaneous Neurofibroma Susceptible to Modification by the Hippo Pathway.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30348677/) (30348677).

## How it grows

The most useful clinical growth data come from volumetric MRI and whole-body MRI cohorts. Growth is heterogeneous and age-dependent. Pediatric and younger patients tend to show faster plexiform neurofibroma growth than adults, while adult internal neurofibromas are often slower or stable over long follow-up: [NF1 plexiform neurofibroma growth rate by volumetric MRI: relationship to age and body weight.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17215493/) (17215493), [Growth dynamics of plexiform neurofibromas: a retrospective cohort study of 201 patients with neurofibromatosis 1.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23035791/) (23035791), and [Ten-Year Follow-up of Internal Neurofibroma Growth Behavior in Adult Patients With Neurofibromatosis Type 1 Using Whole-Body MRI.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36332985/) (36332985). Whole-body MRI provides tumor-burden observables for calibration: [Assessment of benign tumor burden by whole-body MRI in patients with neurofibromatosis 1.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18559970/) (18559970). Growth is also clinically meaningful because volume change can relate to morbidity: [Association of plexiform neurofibroma volume changes and development of clinical morbidities in neurofibromatosis 1.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29718344/) (29718344).

Distinct nodular lesions and atypical neurofibromas should not be collapsed into the same state as diffuse benign plexiform mass. Longitudinal imaging separates nodular lesion behavior from broader PN growth: [Longitudinal evaluation of peripheral nerve sheath tumors in neurofibromatosis type 1: growth analysis of plexiform neurofibromas and distinct nodular lesions.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32152628/) (32152628). Atypical neurofibromas are premalignant intermediates, with CDKN2A/B deletion and other genomic/pathologic changes marking progression risk: [Atypical neurofibromas in neurofibromatosis type 1 are premalignant tumors.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21987445/) (21987445) and [The characteristics of 76 atypical neurofibromas as precursors to neurofibromatosis 1 associated malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumors.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29409029/) (29409029). Overall benign tumor burden is also associated with MPNST risk: [Benign whole body tumor volume is a risk factor for malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumors in neurofibromatosis type 1.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24166582/) (24166582).

## What drives local expansion

The microenvironment is not optional. Nf1+/- bone marrow and c-kit-dependent components are required in classic models: [Nf1-dependent tumors require a microenvironment containing Nf1+/-- and c-kit-dependent bone marrow.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18984156/) (18984156). Mast cells, macrophages, fibroblasts, blood vessels, neurons, and ECM contribute signals and structure: [Mast cells and the neurofibroma microenvironment.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20233971/) (20233971), [Nf1+/- mast cells induce neurofibroma like phenotypes through secreted TGF-beta signaling.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16835260/) (16835260), [Neurofibroma-associated macrophages play roles in tumor growth and response to pharmacological inhibition.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23099891/) (23099891), and [Contributions of inflammation and tumor microenvironment to neurofibroma tumorigenesis.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29596064/) (29596064). Neuronal activity is also a plausible growth input: peripheral neuron activity-regulated COL1A2 increased proliferation of NF1-deficient Schwann cells in NF1 models: [Neuronal hyperexcitability drives central and peripheral nervous system tumor progression in models of neurofibromatosis-1.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35589737/) (35589737).

## Physical modeling implications

A physical model should not be a simple radially expanding sphere unless the goal is only a toy baseline. The literature supports a hybrid or agent-based model on a nerve/plexus graph embedded in tissue:

1. NF1-deficient Schwann-lineage tumor cells as the initiating and proliferating agents.
2. Nf1+/- fibroblasts, mast cells, macrophages, endothelial cells, and neurons as active microenvironment agents or fields.
3. ECM as both a biochemical and mechanical field, with collagen/basement membrane composition and stiffness affecting growth, invasion, and MEK-inhibitor response.
4. Anisotropic growth along nerve fascicles/branches rather than isotropic free-space growth.
5. MRI-observed tumor volumes as patient-scale calibration targets, and 3D cultures/organoids as local parameter-calibration systems.

ECM and mechanics have direct supporting evidence. Single-cell matrisome work maps matrix components and matrix-producing cells in cutaneous neurofibromas: [Human cutaneous neurofibroma matrisome revealed by single-cell RNA sequencing.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33413690/) (33413690). Basement-membrane ECM proteins relate to development and MEK response: [Basement membrane proteins in extracellular matrix characterize NF1 neurofibroma development and response to MEK inhibitor.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37140985/) (37140985). 3D plexiform neurofibroma models and fibroblast secretome experiments show that spheroid growth and invasiveness can be studied in controlled matrices: [Development of 3D culture models of plexiform neurofibroma and initial application for phenotypic characterization and drug screening.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29055717/) (29055717) and [A Fibroblast-Derived Secretome Stimulates the Growth and Invasiveness of 3D Plexiform Neurofibroma Spheroids.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39061138/) (39061138). The most direct mechanobiology paper in this package reports that ECM stiffening promotes growth/invasion phenotypes and reduced selumetinib sensitivity in 3D plexiform neurofibroma cultures: [Mechanical Stiffening Promotes Growth, Invasion-Associated Phenotypes, and Reduced Selumetinib Sensitivity in 3D Plexiform Neurofibroma Cultures](https://www.mdpi.com/2073-4409/15/10/877) (10.3390/cells15100877).

## Model systems available

Useful physical/experimental systems include immortalized normal and NF1 neurofibroma Schwann cells ([Immortalization of human normal and NF1 neurofibroma Schwann cells.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27617404/) (27617404)), pharmacogenomic profiling resources ([Pharmacological and genomic profiling of neurofibromatosis type 1 plexiform neurofibroma-derived schwann cells.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29893754/) (29893754)), 3D plexiform culture systems ([Development of 3D culture models of plexiform neurofibroma and initial application for phenotypic characterization and drug screening.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29055717/) (29055717)), iPSC and humanized neurofibroma models ([Reprogramming Captures the Genetic and Tumorigenic Properties of Neurofibromatosis Type 1 Plexiform Neurofibromas.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30713041/) (30713041), [Humanized neurofibroma model from induced pluripotent stem cells delineates tumor pathogenesis and developmental origins.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33108355/) (33108355), [Modeling iPSC-derived human neurofibroma-like tumors in mice uncovers the heterogeneity of Schwann cells within plexiform neurofibromas.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35172160/) (35172160)), and patient-derived cutaneous neurofibroma organoids ([A platform for rapid patient-derived cutaneous neurofibroma organoid establishment and screening.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38744290/) (38744290)). For cutaneous neurofibroma model selection, see [Existing and Developing Preclinical Models for Neurofibromatosis Type 1-Related Cutaneous Neurofibromas.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37330719/) (37330719).

## Biggest gaps

The literature has strong pieces but not a single definitive physical simulator. The biggest missing calibration data are patient-specific tissue mechanics, serial ECM composition, local nerve topology at tumor initiation, cell-density maps through time, and matched MRI-to-histology datasets. A practical first model should therefore use MRI growth rates for macro-scale validation and 3D culture/organoid data for local rules.
